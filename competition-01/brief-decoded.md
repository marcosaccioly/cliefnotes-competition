# Brief decoded — Competition #01 (Ruff Cuts)

Breaking down the brief before writing anything. Separates what's said, what's implied, and what the judges (the community) will actually reward.

## The client (Dana)

- 2 years running Ruff Cuts in Austin. 15 years as a vet tech before that.
- Founded the business out of **ethical frustration**: burned out watching dogs tremble in clinics. Ruff Cuts is the answer to that discomfort — mobile van, groom at home, zero stress.
- 3 vans, 3 groomers, fully booked 6 weeks out. Status: **small, respected, demand-saturated**.
- She is the brand's technical authority. The voice she wants codified is hers.

**Important subtext:** Dana isn't a solopreneur trying to look big. She is a senior professional with *opinions* — she has a problem with the pet industry's cute-baby-talk default and wants explicit distance from it.

## Her audience

- Dual-income households, 35-55.
- **Time-poor, cash-OK.** They pay $95-140 for home service.
- Many work from home. The rhythm: van arrives → groomer texts when done → owner picks up the dog.
- **Talk to them like smart adults** — explicit in the brief. They don't want infantilized language.

## Explicit problem vs. subtext

**Explicit:** "I need to hire someone for social / email, but I can't explain how we talk."

**Subtext:** fully-booked-6-weeks-out plus an imminent hire equals **brand dilution risk at scale**. Dana knows voice is a fragile asset and that getting this wrong can erode the trust she's built over 2 years. The voice guide is an insurance policy, not a marketing document.

## Voice territory (as literally requested)

**YES:** warm, professional, genuine love of dogs, smart-adult register, calm confidence.
**NO:** cutesy, "fur baby", "pawsitively", puns, baby-talk, stiff, corporate.
**Vibe:** *"your friend who happens to be an expert"* — premium without snobbery, professional without corporateness.

## Deliverable explicitly requested

What she listed — all of it must be covered:

1. Tone
2. Phrases we'd use / phrases we'd never use
3. How we talk about competitors (no trash-talk, but clearly different)
4. How we handle complaints
5. How we write a **caption** vs. an **apology email** (different formats)

If any of these is missing from the guide, the submission is incomplete.

## Competitive lens (Clief Notes)

The vote comes from the community, which is **AI-first**. Two decision axes for the voter:

- **Axis 1 — Nice output?** Everyone will submit something nice. Doesn't differentiate.
- **Axis 2 — Transparent system?** Few will show *how* they got there and *how AI reproduces it*. That's the axis that decides.

The brief itself screams the invitation: *"how you get AI to nail the tone... that's all on you"*.

## Constraints

- Submission is a comment on the post: link + short text works better than a PDF pasted in.
- Deadline: Monday, April 27th, 12:00 PM EST (hard).
- Our chosen format: single-file HTML → GitHub Pages. View / Study toggle.

## Non-goals (things we will NOT do)

- No listing 20 adjectives ("warm, friendly, knowledgeable, passionate...") — adjective soup is generic.
- No mascot or named persona — Dana didn't ask and it clashes with "talk to smart adults".
- No agency jargon ("brand pillars / archetypal wheel / tone matrix") — sounds like a bought template.
- No pretentious manifesto copy ("we believe in a world where...") — premium, not performative.
- No hiding the AI system as "secret sauce" — transparency *is* the differentiator.
